About the project

Why 52 Weeks?

There are many ways to complain about public life. This project is an attempt to do something more useful.

A Look at Civic Life: 52 Weeks for the Common Good asks whether one ordinary citizen can take one public problem each week, understand it properly, use the existing democratic routes, make one reasonable ask, and document what happens.

Why 52?

The number 52 was chosen because there are 52 weeks in a year. A year is long enough to test a serious civic method, but short enough to stay disciplined. It turns a vague wish to “fix politics” into a practical rhythm: one week, one issue, one evidence base, one public log.

The aim is not to pretend these are the 52 most important problems in the country. They are not. The aim is to create a structured civic challenge: to take issues that affect ordinary life, test them against public evidence, and ask whether reasonable improvements can be pursued through the routes that already exist.

This is not a party-political project. It is also not neutral in the sense of having no values.

The project is concerned with human dignity, truthfulness, fair work, public accountability, practical competence, care for the vulnerable, responsible use of public money and the common good. It is influenced by Catholic social teaching, but it is not a religious pressure group. It is a civic project, open to people of goodwill who want public life to be more readable, more answerable and more fair.

The weekly method

  1. Define the problem in plain English.
  2. Check whether a route or workflow already exists.
  3. Gather public evidence.
  4. Make one reasonable ask.
  5. Contact the relevant people or organisations through the proper route.
  6. Log what happened.

Why the order matters

The 52 weeks are arranged as a ladder. The project begins with the basics: readable documents, decision ownership, public questions, honest consultations, information rights, procurement transparency, AI accountability and digital exclusion.

These may sound boring. They matter because ordinary people cannot participate in public life if they cannot understand what is being decided, who owns the decision, where the money goes, or how to respond.

Only after that foundation does the project move into harder issues: poverty pay, public contracts, low-paid public-service work, social care, workplace dignity, welfare, debt, housing, transport, skills, health, justice and climate resilience.

The logic is cumulative. If public bodies must explain decisions clearly, they should also show who owns them. If ownership is visible, citizens can ask better questions. If questions are better, consultations must be more honest. If decision-making becomes clearer, public spending can be followed. If spending can be followed, contracts can be scrutinised. If contracts can be scrutinised, pay, conditions, welfare, housing and public value can be discussed more honestly.

It is not a list of complaints. It is a route from readability to accountability to fairness.

What counts as progress?

The project will not measure success only by whether every ask is achieved. Progress may mean a clearer webpage, a better signpost, a councillor asking a question, an existing process being explained, an error being corrected, or a weak proposal being reworked into something more useful.

A failed route can still be useful if it is documented honestly. It helps the next person understand where the barrier lies.

What this is not

This project is not a political party, a substitute for existing campaign groups, a professional newsroom, a legal service, a personal casework service, or a platform for attacking public servants.

It is a public civic field journal. It begins from a simple belief: public life should be readable to ordinary people. Once it is readable, it can be made answerable. Once it is answerable, it has a better chance of becoming fair.

That is why 52 weeks.
