About the project

Why 52 Weeks?

There are many ways to complain about public life. This project is an attempt to do something more useful.

A Look at Civic Life: 52 Weeks for the Common Good began with a simple question: could one ordinary citizen take one public problem each week, understand it properly, use the existing democratic routes, make one reasonable ask, and document what happens?

The number 52 was chosen because there are 52 weeks in a year. A year is long enough to test a serious civic method, but short enough to remain disciplined. It prevents the project from becoming a vague wish to “fix politics” and turns it into a practical rhythm: one week, one issue, one evidence base, one public log.

The aim is not to pretend that 52 topics are the 52 most important problems in the country. That would be dishonest. The aim is to create a structured civic challenge: to take significant problems that affect ordinary life, test them against evidence, and ask whether reasonable improvements can be pursued through the routes that already exist.

This is not a party-political project. It is also not neutral in the sense of having no values.

The project is concerned with human dignity, truthfulness, fair work, public accountability, practical competence, care for the vulnerable, responsible use of public money, and the common good. It is influenced by Catholic social teaching and by recent Church concern about artificial intelligence, human responsibility, social justice and the proper use of technology. But it is not a religious pressure group. It is a civic project, open to people of goodwill who want public life to be more readable, more answerable and more fair.

The basic method

Each week follows the same basic pattern.

First, the problem is defined in plain English. If the issue cannot be explained clearly, it is not ready to be campaigned on.

Second, the project checks whether a solution or workflow already exists. Many public problems are not caused by a complete absence of policy. Sometimes the law already exists but is not enforced. Sometimes the guidance exists but is hidden. Sometimes the process exists but is unintelligible to ordinary people. Sometimes another organisation is already doing good work and should be credited rather than duplicated.

Third, the evidence is gathered. The project uses public documents, official guidance, council papers, parliamentary material, inspection reports, datasets, consultation documents, published policies and serious civil-society evidence. Artificial intelligence may help read, summarise and organise material, but it is not evidence in itself. Responsibility remains human.

Fourth, one reasonable ask is made. Not ten demands. Not a manifesto. One ask that can be understood, tested, accepted, refused, improved or redirected.

Fifth, the relevant people or organisations are contacted through the proper route. That may be a councillor, an MP, a public consultation, a council public forum, a Freedom of Information request, a charity, a professional body, a regulator, a public committee, or an existing campaign group.

Sixth, the response is logged. If there is progress, that is recorded. If there is no reply, that is recorded. If the original ask proves too broad or badly framed, it is corrected. If a better route is found, the project says so.

This is why the project is built around accountability as well as campaigning. It is not enough to say that public bodies should be accountable. A citizen-led project must also be accountable. That means showing the sources used, the questions asked, the responses received, the mistakes corrected and the compromises accepted.

How the 52 topics were chosen

The 52 topics were chosen using several tests.

The first is the common-good test. Does the issue affect human dignity, fair work, family and community life, public accountability, social trust, the vulnerable, or the responsible use of public money?

The second is the evidence test. Is there enough public material to show that the issue is real rather than merely a personal irritation?

The third is the campaignability test. Can an ordinary citizen do something lawful and practical about it: ask a question, submit evidence, write to a representative, use a consultation, explain a process, or support an existing organisation?

The fourth is the cross-line test. Can the issue be understood by people of different political instincts? The project is non-party, not non-moral. It is interested in good ideas from the left, right, centre, local communities, churches, unions, charities, businesses and ordinary citizens.

The fifth is the practical-lever test. Is there a point of action? A policy, contract, committee, budget, inspection regime, reporting habit, guidance note, public meeting, procurement rule, or administrative process that could be improved?

The topics are therefore not random. They are issues where public life, money, services, work, housing, welfare, health, transport, accountability or civic participation seem to meet a practical point of action.

Why the order matters

The 52 weeks are arranged as a ladder.

The project begins with low-resistance civic foundations: plain-English public documents, decision ownership, public question rights, honest consultations, Freedom of Information and Subject Access accountability, procurement transparency, public-sector AI accountability and digital exclusion safeguards.

These are not glamorous issues. They are deliberately boring. But they matter because ordinary people cannot participate in public life if they cannot understand what is being decided, who owns the decision, where the money goes, or how to respond.

Only after that foundation does the project move into harder issues: poverty pay, public contracts, low-paid public-service work, social care, workplace dignity, welfare, debt, housing, transport, skills, health, justice and climate resilience.

The logic is cumulative. If public bodies must explain decisions clearly, then they should also show who owns them. If ownership is visible, citizens can ask better questions. If questions are better, consultations must be more honest. If consultation and decision-making become clearer, public spending can be followed. If spending can be followed, contracts can be scrutinised. If contracts can be scrutinised, pay, conditions, welfare, housing and public value can be discussed more honestly.

That is the point of the 52-week structure. It is not a list of complaints. It is a route from readability to accountability to fairness.

What counts as progress?

The project will not measure success only by whether every ask is achieved. That would be unrealistic.

Progress may mean a public body improving a webpage, a councillor asking a question, an existing process being clarified, an error being corrected, a better organisation being identified, or a weak proposal being reconfigured into something more workable.

A failed route can still be useful if it is documented honestly. It helps the next person understand where the barrier lies.

Success may look small from the outside. A clearer public document. A better signpost. A published answer. A corrected claim. A new contact. A more honest consultation. A public log showing who owns a decision. These are not revolutions. But public life is often made worse by accumulated confusion, drift and evasion. It can also be improved by accumulated clarity.

The hope is that five or six meaningful improvements over a year would be worthwhile, and that even the weeks that do not produce change would leave behind a useful record for other citizens.

How the project handles bias

This project is run by one person, assisted by artificial intelligence. That creates an obvious risk.

One person brings personal experience, instincts, frustrations, hopes and blind spots. AI systems can also mirror the user too readily, produce confident summaries, smooth over uncertainty, or make a preferred argument sound more complete than it really is.

The project will not pretend that these risks do not exist. It will deal with them openly.

The aim is not to be “unbiased” in the impossible sense of having no values. The project has values: human dignity, truthfulness, fair work, public accountability, practical competence, care for the vulnerable, and the common good. It is non-party, but it is not morally empty.

The aim is to be bias-aware, source-led and accountable.

Each campaign will therefore include a bias and accountability check. Before making an ask, the project will identify its starting assumption, the evidence supporting it, the evidence that may challenge it, the strongest reasonable opposing argument, and the conditions under which the ask should be changed, softened or withdrawn.

Artificial intelligence will be used as a civic assistant, not as an authority. It may help summarise documents, compare arguments, generate questions, structure evidence and make public information more readable. It will not be treated as evidence in itself. Published claims must rest on sources that can be checked.

The project will also distinguish between fact, inference and opinion. A fact is something directly supported by a source. An inference is a reasoned conclusion drawn from available evidence. An opinion is a judgement. These should not be blurred.

Where possible, the project will contact the relevant public body, representative, organisation or stakeholder before drawing a firm conclusion. If a process already exists and works, the project will say so. If the original ask was wrong, too broad or badly framed, the project will correct it.

How AI will be used

AI will be used carefully and openly.

It may help to summarise long documents, identify possible questions, compare public statements with source material, structure evidence, draft plain-English explanations, prepare contact lists, generate video scripts, or turn a campaign page into a reusable briefing.

It will not be used to invent facts, impersonate people, mass-email public officials, generate outrage, or replace human judgement. It will not be treated as a witness. It will not be allowed to turn one person’s instinct into a false certainty.

The project’s working rule is simple: AI may assist the citizen, but the citizen remains accountable.

That matters because the public argument around artificial intelligence is often poor. Some people treat AI as magic. Others treat it as moral contamination. Neither position is good enough.

This project takes a more practical view. Technology should be judged by whether it serves the human person, strengthens responsibility, improves truthfulness, widens participation, and supports the common good. If AI helps ordinary citizens understand public life better, it can be useful. If it makes public life more manipulative, noisy or irresponsible, it should be resisted.

The purpose is not to celebrate AI for its own sake. The purpose is to show that modern tools can be used calmly, responsibly and in service of real civic participation.

How the project avoids harassment

The project will not encourage dogpiling. It will not publish private contact details. It will not treat every delay as corruption or every disagreement as bad faith.

It will criticise systems, decisions and policies before individuals. It will give people and organisations a fair chance to respond. It will use formal routes before escalation. It will not make allegations that cannot be evidenced. It will welcome corrections. It will accept reasonable compromise.

This is important because civic persistence can easily become civic aggression. That is not the aim. The aim is to be boringly reasonable and relentlessly persistent, not theatrical or hostile.

Public servants, councillors, MPs, charity workers and campaigners are human beings. Some will be helpful. Some will be overworked. Some will disagree. Some may ignore the project. The response should remain proportionate.

The project is not designed to shame individuals. It is designed to make public systems easier to understand, question and improve.

The first stage

The first stage is a pilot.

Week 0 is preparation. It is for setting up the website, email, project method, campaign tracker, public log, corrections process and video format.

The first four campaign weeks are deliberately foundational.

Week 1 asks whether public documents can be made readable to ordinary citizens.

Week 2 asks whether major decisions can show who owns them.

Week 3 asks whether citizens’ public question rights can be made easier to understand and use.

Week 4 asks whether consultations can be made more honest about what is already decided, what remains open, and what evidence could change the outcome.

Only after that pilot will the wider 52-week programme continue. The pilot exists to test the method before making larger claims.

What the project is not

This project is not a political party. It is not a substitute for existing campaign groups. It is not a professional newsroom. It is not a legal service. It is not a personal casework service. It is not an AI demonstration for its own sake. It is not a platform for attacking public servants. It is not a promise to solve the country in a year.

It is a public civic field journal. It tests whether an ordinary citizen can use public evidence, existing routes and responsible AI to pursue practical common-good improvements, one issue at a time.

What the project hopes to prove

The project hopes to prove that citizenship does not have to mean shouting into the void.

It may mean reading the document. Finding the responsible body. Checking the existing process. Asking the clear question. Making the reasonable request. Logging the answer. Correcting the mistake. Trying the next route. Explaining the result.

That sounds small. It may be small. But a country in which ordinary people can understand public decisions, ask better questions and see where responsibility lies is a healthier country than one in which public life is only readable to insiders.

If, after a year, all that exists is a public record of 52 well-sourced attempts to understand and improve civic life, that will still be worthwhile.

If five of those attempts lead to practical improvements, that will be significant.

If others can copy the method locally, that will matter more than any single campaign.

The project begins from a simple belief: public life should be readable to ordinary people. Once it is readable, it can be made answerable. Once it is answerable, it has a better chance of becoming fair.

That is why 52 weeks.
