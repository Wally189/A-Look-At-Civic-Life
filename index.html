Can ordinary people in the United Kingdom still engage in public life?

We are surrounded by public information, consultations, announcements, dashboards, strategies and decisions. But can a normal person still work out what is happening, who is responsible, and what lawful route exists to ask for something better?

Make public life readable. Then make it answerable. Then make it fair.

The question behind the project

Many people feel that public bodies consult, publish and explain — but often in ways that still leave ordinary citizens outside the room.

There is too much information, too much disinformation, too much jargon, and too little plain explanation of who owns a decision and what can actually be changed.

This project tests that problem in public. Each week, I take one civic issue and ask: what is the route for a layperson?

What happens each week

I pick one practical issue — a public document, council route, consultation, NHS question, public contract, hardship scheme, transport problem or democratic process — and follow the evidence.

  • What is the problem in plain English?
  • Who appears to own it?
  • What public document or route proves that?
  • What is the smallest reasonable ask?
  • What happens when an ordinary citizen uses the route properly?

Not a party campaign

This is not about joining a party, shouting online or pretending every public servant is useless. It is about testing whether civic life still works for people who are not insiders.

The tone is deliberately calm: read the source, find the route, ask the question, log the response, correct mistakes, and keep going reasonably.
