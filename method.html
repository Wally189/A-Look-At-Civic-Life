Method and safeguards

How the project works

Each week follows the same simple test: can a layperson read the evidence, find the route, ask the right question and understand the answer?

The method is deliberately boring. That is the point. Public life should not depend on outrage, insider language or party loyalty. It should be possible for a citizen to follow a route calmly and see what happens.

The weekly test

  1. Choose one practical civic question.
  2. Put the problem into plain English.
  3. Check whether a route already exists.
  4. Read the public evidence.
  5. Make one reasonable ask.
  6. Use the proper route: councillor, MP, consultation, FOI, public question, regulator, ombudsman or published contact point.
  7. Record what happened.
  8. Accept reasonable compromise, correct mistakes and reconfigure the question if needed.

The first rule

Do not invent a new campaign before checking whether a route already exists.

Bias and accountability check

Before making an ask, the project should name its starting assumption and test it against the evidence.

  • What is the claim?
  • What source supports it?
  • What source might challenge it?
  • What is the strongest reasonable opposing argument?
  • Is this fact, inference or opinion?
  • What would make the ask change, soften or be withdrawn?

Responsible AI rule

Artificial intelligence may assist the citizen, but it must not become the authority.

  • AI may help read, summarise and organise material.
  • AI may help draft questions, scripts and briefings.
  • AI is not evidence.
  • Published claims must rest on checkable sources.
  • Human judgement remains responsible for what is published.

What counts as a result?

A result might be a full change, a partial improvement, a clearer explanation, a better signpost, a useful refusal, an evidence gap, or simply a public record of which route did and did not work.

How the tone is kept sane

  • Criticise systems, decisions and policies before individuals.
  • Give people a fair chance to respond.
  • Do not publish private contact details.
  • Do not encourage dogpiling.
  • Separate fact, inference and opinion.
  • Correct errors publicly.
  • Use formal routes before escalation.
  • Keep the tone civil even when the issue is serious.

Civic directory

This project should not pretend to be a new institution. Where good tools or organisations already exist, the right thing is to use them, credit them and route readers towards them.
